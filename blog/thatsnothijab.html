That's not hijab!

By Zareen & Selina | Jan 18, 2016

We explore the darker side of our community. THAT'S NOT HIJAB. We see the phrase flying around social media and in this case out of the mouths of brothers.

“To handle yourself, use your head; to handle others, use your heart.”

- ' Eleanor Roosevelt '

To share your 'That's not hijab' story, please email selina@amaliah.co.uk

Selina

Bismillah

I am Selina from Amaliah and about four years ago I started my hijab journey. I used to dress differently to how I do now and honestly didn’t know much about Islam sadly. Alhamdulilah now I am learning more each day about hijab and more. Here’s my account of when I first heard the phrase “ That’s not Hijab “

As I walked to the mosque unsure and unconfident about my hijab, which I'd spent ages in front of the mirror trying to get 'right', a YouTube tutorial helped, it was summer and hot too so I 'felt' it.

As I walked to the mosque to meet a sister I passed the first entrance and heard a brother say "that's not hijab" I was so upset, I'd tried so hard, did he know today was my first day?

Did he realise that I felt like taking it off and giving up because of what he said? No, he didn't realise the implications of his words and that advice should also be delivered in a certain way. Alhamdulilah, I stayed strong with Allah’s guidance and five years on, I still wear it and still face struggles. I see this sentence flying around on social media all the time and although I welcome sincere advice, sometimes the way the message is conveyed is often in isolation without knowing the background of the person, their journey, intention and so much more.

The beauty of your intention is that it is between you and Allah only - Unknown

Zareen

I have been currently wearing the hijab for about 7-8 years but before I decided to wear it on a full time basis, I would use the Holy Month of Ramadan as an opportunity to wear it fully and try to understand the changes that came with it.

I come from a conservative background but not a religious one, so it was a really big deal for me as an individual, to even contemplate taking the hijab on. At the time I had no family or close friends who wore the hijab, and I was in the midst of working at a very trendy makeup brand where there was a certain amount of pressure to constantly look and be a fashion trendsetter.

One particular Ramadan, I decided to wear the hijab for the entire month and made sure I performed my 5 daily prayers.

It caught all of my colleagues by surprise as there was no hijabi that worked in any of the Brand’s counters through out the entire country! Once Ramadan was over, I took off the hijab and went back to my regular dress code of skinny jeans, sleeveless tops etc. The following year, I once again decided to wear the hijab during Ramadan, but this time round, I felt a stronger connection with the hijab. Nonetheless, when Ramadan was over, I took it off again and went back to my regular self.

A few weeks after Ramadan, I remember serving two young girls at the Makeup store I worked at, and I could hear them murmur something about me in Arabic. Once they left the store, I asked one of my Arab colleagues to translate what they said as she was standing right by them.

She said “Zareen ,they were making fun of you. They say how come you only wear hijab in Ramadan and then after Ramadan you dress like model? That’s not hijab” she said in her thick Syrian accent. I nodded, bit my lip and tried to brush off the feeling of frustration, and went back to work.

Alhamdulilah, the following year, I was already wearing the hijab with confidence and conviction before Ramadan came. And yes, during my ’trial’ period I had friends, colleagues, and clients judge me, some even went to extra lengths to criticize my actions or lack thereof. And yes I’ve been called a “Ramadan Muslim” amongst many other names, but luckily for me, I didn’t let other people’s judgments and opinions affect my final decision.

Yes, at one point in my life, I only did “Muslim” things such as praying, giving up music, covering up, only during Ramadan. So what ? We all have to start somewhere!

Some of us might be stronger than others and pull through without hesitation or doubt, and there are others like me, that stumbled and needed time to reflect and prepare oneself. So instead of pointing fingers when a sister or brother is down or confused, give them the encouragement, advice and motivation to stand up. Ask them what their concerns or fears are. Leave the ‘judgement’ up to God.

I was, officially the first hijabi at the Makeup brand I worked with, and when I decided to leave the brand 6 months down the line, Alhamdulilah 3 other Makeup artists put on the hijab as well. It’s as if, even they needed an extra push to make that shift in their lives.
